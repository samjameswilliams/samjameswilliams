## Good practice in reviewing workshop - Celebrating Engineering Technicians  

Malcolm Peake  

Technicians are professionally competent practitioners. There is a need to recognise and
celebrate the contribution of technicians.  

**The French Revolution (1789-1799)**

* The metric system (1791)  
* Semaphore telegraph (1792)  
* Hot air balloons - Compagnie d'Aérostiers (1794)  
* Engineering schools - École Polytechnique (1794)  
* The kilogramme (1795)  
* The graphite pencil (1795)  

Marc Brunel came to the UK and was a leading light in the UK Industrial Revolution.  

We know the names of the engineers but not the technicians. 

![victorian-technicians](image-9.png)

Industrial revolution led to increased urbanisation. Initially living conditions were
very poor but Basilgette and others made it possible for large urban conurbations to be
healthier/cleaner.  

![engineering-technicians](image-10.png)  

In 1968 SCET was formed. Ultimately in 1989 it merged into the 
ICE. Technician status is often seen as a stepping stone to Engineer, but to many it isn't, and 
should it be? Many are happy in the technician role.  

Many thought the emergence of CAD would spell the end of technicians in Civil 
Engineering, but it hasn't. They're as necessary as ever.

The technician community must be celebrated and respected.  

Professional qualification raises their status and importance in the industry.  

![pathways-to-recognition](image-11.png)  

It is possible for technicians to cross over into engineering. But not all will or want
to. Some junior engineers opt to go into a technician role because of their interest in
the technology (namely advanced BIM and AI).  

What do technicians do?

### Building bridges between engineers and technicians  

![typical-roles](image-19.png)  

Activities: 
1. What would these people do in a typical day?  
Room 4 - Focus on 4 H&S, 6 Communications  

6 - Communications:  
* quality checks on drawing output  
* model integrity for export to FEA, CFD  
* reinforcement detailing to IStructE standards  

4 - H&S:  
* raise concerns with unsafe aspects of design  
* clash detection  

2. What questions should we ask?  

* What H&S legislation is relevant to your work?  
* What is not compliant with H&S laws?  
* What is reasonable to build? How would you build it?  
* What would you do if you see something unsafe in the design?  
* Have you ever challenged the engineers design?  
* What are the most important things for making drawings clear?  
* What does a good/bad drawing look like?  
* How do you communicate hazards?  

Consensus that asking questions on compensation events and contractual issues is unfair.
Awareness of contractual issues may be expected but not detailed knowledge.  

* Has there been a time you've reduced the environmental impact of your project?  
* Were all the necessary permits in place? Did we check?  

Asset management:  
* Why test concrete?  
* When should you strip formwork? Why keep registers?  
* How does the asset work you're inspecting? What does it look like when it goes wrong?  
* How do you resolve conflict?  
* How do you manage your time?  
* How did you go about understanding the asset you're inspecting?  
* How do you ensure you're inspecting the right asset?  
* How did you resolve an access issue for an inspection?  

Academic:  
* management of waste in the lab?  
* keeping data from experiments safe?  

The ICE is doing more outreach events for technicians.

## Industry developments: Improving productivity in the industry  

Ed McCann  

**What is the point of civil engineering?**  

Think about the UNSDGs, we work towards providing those.  

Productivity is closely related to efficiency

>"It would be well if engineering were less generally thought of, and
 even defined, as the art of constructing. In a certain important sense
 it is rather the art of not constructing; or, to define it rudely but not
 inaptly, it is the art of doing that well with one dollar, which any
 bungler can do with two after a fashion."
*Arthur Mellen Wellington*

![AMW](image-21.png)  

Lessons from Toyota:  
* Effectiveness - Focus on delivering value to the customer  
* Efficiency - Get rid of waste  

![alt text](image-22.png)  

The government definition of productivity $\neq$ the ICE's definition  

Productivity improvement is about continuously
improving the processes we use.

To improve processes, we change the volume or quality
of the capital inputs and/or improve how we use them.

For example, we can deploy better trained and more
capable human capital, manage and arrange them
differently, incentivise them to use manufactured capital
(technology) more effectively.

Think about the Government Green Book to see what government values.  

Social capital is massively underestimated in Civil Engineering. 

![optimal-value-product](image-23.png)

The first step is to find the minimum viable product, bare minimum to work. Add nice to
haves until the return on investment diminishes.  

Biggest value killer (one contracts manager): Reporting and paperwork

> "within five years, the construction industry should deliver its
 products to its customers in the same way as the best
 consumer-led manufacturing and service industries. To achieve
 the dramatic increases in efficiency and quality that are both
 possible and necessary we must all rethink construction"
*1998, Sir John Egan*  

Less than 0.5% of infrastructure projects are on time and to budget.  

![ineffective-high-security-gate](image-24.png) 
*Ineffective security gate*

Over specification in buildings lead to 30-60% more material than needed.  

That there are a lot of issues,
including:

* Fragmentation

There's a temptation to trust gurus ... beware.

**PAS 4010 Productivity management for infrastructure projects** on the way.
Conforming projects:
* carry out a systematic analysis of the drivers of productivity within
  their project / program and to identify key risks and opportunities to
  productivity  
* produce a plan to manage the risks and opportunities  
* ensure that the plan is delivered  

**Exercise**
How can we get engineers focused on improving productivity within the industry?  

How does innovation affect productivity? Any examples?  

What productivity related questions could you ask candidates at each level of review
for the attributes assigned to your group? Try to consider appropriate questions to ask
of EngTech, IEng and CEng candidates (Groups will focus on different attributes)  

* Do you know where waste is coming from on your project?  
* Can engineers do some of what BIM technician's do?  
* Standard design that is less efficient, or bespoke design(s) as lean as possible?  
* What is the motivation for consultants to design less if they're paid by the hour?  
Ask candidates what they might do differently to their managers. Decisions above their 
position.  
* What are the risks associated with innovation?  
* Can we use AI to calculate quantities? Why or why not?  

Understand production systems. How do materials move through the whole system?  
Get designers and contractors together early. But it has to be people with the right 
knowledge. 1st: what are you trying to do? 2nd: how are you going to do it?  
McCann: Mistake when ICE removed the 1 year on site requirement for designers. Site
engineers also need to go into the design office.  

Change takes courage.  

> Technology goes after expensive jobs that are repetitive and mechanisable.  
*Ed McCann (paraphrase)*   

![changing-to-compete](image-27.png)  
There's a lot of variation in the construction industry around productivity. Getting our
worst performing areas to our best is almost a doubling in productivity.  

Innovations don't always bring the promised improvements. BIM can be a case in point. In
many areas it has made things slower and more expensive. Know why you're doing something
before you do it. We often under-deploy our best ideas.
*Ed McCann (paraphrase)*  

>‘To do our work, we all have to read a mass of papers. Nearly all of them are far too long.
  This wastes time, while energy has to be spent in looking for the essential points.
  I ask my colleagues and their staff to see to it that their reports are shorter.
  The aim should be reports which set out the main points in a series of short, crisp paragraphs.
  If a report relies on detailed analysis of some complicated factors, or on statistics, these should be set out in an appendix.
  Often the occasion is best met by submitting not a full-dress report, but an ‘aide-memoire’ consisting of headings only, which can be expanded orally if needed.
  Let us have an end of such phrases as these:
  ‘It is also of importance to bear in mind the following considerations’, or ‘Consideration should be given to the possibility of carrying into effect’. Most of these woolly phrases are mere padding, which can be left out altogether, or replaced by a single word. Let us not shrink from using the short expressive phrase, even if it is conversational.
  Reports drawn up on the lines I propose may first seem rough as compared with the flat surface of officialese jargon. But the saving in time will be great, while the discipline of setting out the real points concisely will prove an aid to clearer thinking.’  

*Sir Winston Churchill, 9 August 1940*  

In manufacturing people talk about standard work, not products.  
Foster an environment where people can challenge each other and are focussed on the 
work.  
Master-planning is where things fall down more than at the more detailed level. Reduce
